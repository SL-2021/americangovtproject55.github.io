Foundations of American Justice

The Dual Court System

The U.S. judicial system is unique, featuring two parallel court systems: one federal and fifty state systems. This structure, a cornerstone of American federalism, divides legal authority and allows states to tailor laws to their communities while upholding federal law.

This chart illustrates the massive caseload difference, with state courts handling the vast majority of legal disputes in the nation.

Original Jurisdiction

The authority of a court to hear a case for the very first time. This is where the trial happens, evidence is presented, and a factual record is created. It answers the question: "What happened?"

Appellate Jurisdiction

The power of a higher court to review decisions made by lower courts. It does not re-try the case but looks for legal errors. It answers the question: "Was the law applied correctly?"

Trial Courts

The courts of first instance where legal disputes are initially heard and decided. Their primary function is fact-finding: hearing testimony, admitting evidence, and determining what happened. Both federal (U.S. District Courts) and state systems have trial courts.

Appellate Court

A court that hears appeals from a lower court. Its purpose is not to retry a case, but to review the trial court's proceedings for errors of law. They ensure the law was applied fairly and correctly. The U.S. Courts of Appeals are federal appellate courts.

The Federal Court Structure

Established by Article III of the Constitution, the federal judiciary has a clear, three-tiered hierarchy. Its jurisdiction is limited to cases involving the Constitution, federal laws, or specific parties like the U.S. government.

U.S. Supreme Court

The highest court in the federal system and the final arbiter of federal law. Composed of nine justices, it has final appellate jurisdiction over all federal and state court cases involving issues of federal law.

U.S. Court of Appeals

The intermediate level in the federal system. These 13 courts review decisions from the district courts within their circuit to check for legal errors. They do not hold trials.

U.S. District Courts

The trial courts of the federal system. There are 94 federal districts where cases under federal law are first filed, trials are held, and evidence is presented.

U.S. Supreme Court: Discretionary Review

The Supreme Court doesn't hear every case. It uses the "Writ of Certiorari" to select fewer than 100 cases annually from over 7,000 petitions, focusing on those with profound national importance.

A Deep Dive: The Georgia Court System

Georgia provides a fascinating example of a complex, multi-tiered state court system. It features two appellate courts and seven distinct classes of trial courts, each with specialized jurisdiction.

Georgia's Trial Court Landscape

Unlike the unified federal system, Georgia favors specialization. This chart shows the seven types of trial courts and their specific areas of focus, from minor civil claims to the most serious felonies.

Georgia's Appellate Courts

Appeals from trial courts are directed to one of two appellate courts, depending on the subject matter.

State Appeals Courts

These are a state's intermediate appellate courts, serving as the first level of review for most cases. In Georgia, the Court of Appeals of Georgia fulfills this role, handling a high volume of cases to correct legal errors from trial courts.

Supreme Court of Georgia

The state's highest court has exclusive jurisdiction over the most fundamental legal questions, such as constitutionality of laws, election contests, and capital felonies.

Bedrock Constitutional Protections

The entire judicial process rests on guarantees in the U.S. Constitution that ensure fairness and prevent arbitrary government action.

Due Process of Law

A fundamental principle from the 5th and 14th Amendments ensuring fundamental fairness. It guarantees that no one can be deprived of "life, liberty, or property" without the government following fair rules and procedures.

  • Procedural: Guarantees NOTICE and an OPPORTUNITY TO BE HEARD.
  • Substantive: Protects fundamental rights from government interference.

Equal Protection of the Laws

A command from the 14th Amendment requiring that the law be applied impartially. It means similarly situated people must be treated alike, preventing improper government discrimination.

  • Key Idea: Government cannot draw distinctions between people based on irrelevant differences.
